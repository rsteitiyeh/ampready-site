What the PSI exam actually looks like

The Texas journeyman exam is open book, and that phrase misleads more candidates than it helps. Open book does not mean easy. It means the test assumes you have the NEC in front of you and prices its questions accordingly. Four hours sounds generous until you spend six minutes hunting one table because you never learned the code's layout.

The questions lean on grounding and bonding, conductor sizing and ampacity, box and conduit fill, motor circuits, and services and feeders, with a block of Texas-specific licensing law mixed in. You bring your codebook (tabbed, per PSI's rules on permitted markings), and the clock does the rest. We wrote a full piece on the open-book question, including what tabbing is allowed, at AmpExam/is-the-journeyman-electrician-test-open-book.

Passing is 70 percent. Miss it and you pay the exam fee again and reschedule through PSI. That retake fee is the entire financial argument for prepping properly the first time.

One scheduling note from candidates we have talked to: PSI testing centers in the big Texas metros book out, so do not wait until your application is approved to look at dates. Morning slots also tend to go first. A four-hour exam starting at 1 pm after a full morning of nerves is a worse test than the same exam at 8 am, and that is a variable you control for free.

What a Texas-focused prep course should cover

Plenty of generic NEC courses exist. A Texas course earns the word Texas by covering these, in roughly this weighting:

  - Code navigation speed. Timed lookup drills until you can land in any chapter inside 60 seconds. This is half the exam, honestly.

  - Calculations. Branch circuits, feeders, service loads, box fill, conduit fill, voltage drop, motor FLC. Show every step, because the distractor answers are the common arithmetic mistakes.

  - Grounding and bonding, Article 250. The most-tested and most-misunderstood article on every state exam we track.

  - Texas licensing law. TDLR rules, supervision requirements, and the Texas Electrical Safety and Licensing Act questions that generic national courses skip.

  - Full-length timed practice exams. Four hours in a chair with a codebook is a physical skill. Practice it at least twice before test day.

Course formats: what your money buys

Texas prep courses come in three formats, and the price spread is wide. Live classroom courses at trade schools and supply houses run multiple evenings a week for several weeks; you get an instructor who has taken the actual test and classmates to keep you honest. Self-paced online courses cost less and fit around a work schedule, which matters when you are already pulling wire 45 hours a week. Question banks and study guides sit at the bottom of the price range and work fine for disciplined studiers who mainly need reps.

Our take after watching a lot of candidates: format matters less than volume of timed practice. The people who fail usually studied content but never rehearsed the clock. The people who pass on the first attempt almost all took two or more full-length practice exams under real conditions. Buy whichever format guarantees you that, and skip anything that promises actual exam questions, because nobody legitimate has them.

A six-week self-study plan that works

Not everyone needs a paid course. Plenty of hands pass on discipline and a tabbed codebook. Here is the plan we would run with about eight hours a week:

1.  Week 1: Learn the NEC's structure. Chapters 1 through 4 apply generally; 5 through 7 modify them. Tab your book. Do 30 untimed lookups.

2.  Week 2: Article 250 start to finish, plus grounding practice questions until you clear 80 percent.

3.  Week 3: Conductor sizing, ampacity tables, and derating. Table 310.16 and its correction factors should feel like home.

4.  Week 4: Calculations week. Box fill, conduit fill, load calcs, voltage drop, motors. Slowest week for most people, so protect it.

5.  Week 5: Texas licensing law and a first full-length timed practice exam. Score it, then restudy your two weakest areas.

6.  Week 6: Second and third timed practice exams, review misses, schedule with PSI while the material is hot.

FAQ

How many hours do you need to become a journeyman electrician in Texas?

TDLR requires 8,000 hours of on-the-job training under the supervision of a licensed master electrician before you can sit for the journeyman exam. That is roughly four years of full-time work. You register as an apprentice with TDLR first so your hours count. Verify the details on the TDLR electricians page, since rules get amended.

Is the Texas journeyman electrician test open book?

Yes. The PSI-administered exam is open book, and you bring your NEC codebook into the test. You get 4 hours and need 70 percent to pass. Open book rewards candidates who can navigate the code quickly, not ones who memorized it. Check PSI's candidate bulletin for the current rules on tabs and highlighting before test day.

How much does the Texas journeyman exam cost?

You pay TDLR an application fee and PSI an exam fee, and each retake means paying PSI again. We are not printing amounts because they change; check the current amount at https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/electricians.htm before you budget. The bigger financial risk is retake fees from walking in underprepared, which good prep eliminates.

What NEC edition does Texas use for the exam?

Texas adopts NEC editions on its own schedule, and PSI's candidate information bulletin states which edition the exam currently references. Confirm the edition there before buying a codebook or a prep course, and make sure any practice questions you use are written to that same edition. Studying the wrong cycle wastes real points on changed tables.

Bottom line

The Texas journeyman license is 8,000 supervised hours, a TDLR application, and one 4-hour open-book PSI exam at 70 percent. The hours take four years; the exam takes six focused weeks of prep.
