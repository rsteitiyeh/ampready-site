A journeyman electrician exam prep course is a structured program that drills the two skills the licensing exam actually tests: navigating the NEC code book fast, and working calculation problems (conduit fill, box fill, ampacity, voltage drop, service sizing) under a clock. A good one costs $50 to $400, runs 4 to 12 weeks, and centers on timed practice exams.

What the journeyman exam actually tests

Most states contract their exams to PSI or a similar testing vendor. The typical format is 70 to 100 multiple choice questions, 3 to 4 hours, open book with the National Electrical Code as the allowed reference. The current edition is the NEC 2026, published by NFPA (https://www.nfpa.org), though many states still test on 2023 or even 2020. Confirm which edition your state board uses before you buy anything, because a course keyed to the wrong cycle will send you to the wrong article numbers on exam day.

Open book fools people. You cannot look up 90 answers in 240 minutes. The exam is really a speed test on code navigation plus a math test on a dozen calculation types. We cover the open book question in detail at AmpExam/is-the-journeyman-electrician-test-open-book.

Content-wise, nearly every state exam draws from the same well. A prep course that skips any of these is incomplete:

  - Definitions and general requirements (NEC Articles 90, 100, 110)

  - Branch circuits, feeders, and service calculations (Articles 210, 215, 220, 230)

  - Grounding and bonding (Article 250, the heaviest single topic on most exams)

  - Conductor ampacity and adjustment factors (Article 310, Table 310.16)

  - Box fill and conduit fill (314.16, Chapter 9 tables, Annex C)

  - Motors and motor circuits (Article 430, the calculation everyone dreads)

  - Transformers, special occupancies, and low voltage odds and ends

  - Theory: Ohm's law, series and parallel circuits, power factor basics

What a good prep course covers

We judge a course on five things. First, timed full-length practice exams, plural, mapped to your state's question count and time limit. Untimed question banks build knowledge but not pace, and pace is what fails people. Second, code navigation drills: given a question, find the article in under two minutes. Third, worked calculation walkthroughs where the instructor shows the table lookups, not just the answer key. Fourth, the code edition your state tests on. Fifth, some form of diagnostics that tells you which NEC chapters are eating your score.

The wage math justifies doing this right. Per the BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook (https://www.bls.gov/ooh/construction-and-extraction/electricians.htm), the 2024 median pay for electricians was about $62,350 per year, with roughly 9% employment growth projected from 2024 to 2034. Licensed journeymen sit in the upper half of that distribution in most markets. A $300 course that gets you licensed one exam cycle sooner pays for itself in the first week. How to choose a course in five steps

1.  Confirm your state's exam vendor, question count, time limit, and NEC edition on your state board's site. Everything else keys off this.

2.  Take a free diagnostic practice test cold, before buying anything, to find your baseline and your weak chapters.

3.  Shortlist courses that match your NEC edition and offer timed, state-length practice exams. Cut any that will not tell you their question count.

4.  Check that calculations (motor circuits, service sizing, voltage drop, conduit fill) get dedicated modules with worked solutions, not just answer keys.

5.  Pick the format your schedule can actually sustain for 6 to 10 weeks. A cheaper course you finish beats a premium course you abandon in week two.

Self-study vs a paid course

Honest answer: most working electricians can pass with disciplined self-study. You already know the material from the field; the exam mostly checks whether you can find it in the book and do the math. Self-study works when you have four or more years of varied field experience, you can hold yourself to a schedule, and your practice scores start within 15 points of passing.

A paid course earns its money in three situations. Your field experience is narrow (four years of nothing but residential rough-in leaves gaps in motors and services). Your first diagnostic comes back below 55 percent. Or you have failed the exam once already, because a second $75 to $150 retest fee plus lost wages makes course tuition look cheap. A study plan that works regardless of what you buy

Whatever you choose, structure beats intensity. Eight weeks at five hours per week outperforms two frantic weekends. The plan we suggest: weeks 1 and 2 on code layout and tab placement, weeks 3 and 4 on calculations only, weeks 5 and 6 alternating topic drills with timed sections, weeks 7 and 8 on full-length timed exams with review of every miss. Tab your code book as you go; most states allow permanent tabs and a bound book with your own highlights.

One thing we cannot verify for every reader: some states restrict what you can write in your code book. Check your candidate bulletin, because proctors do confiscate books with loose notes.

How a good course teaches calculations: a worked example

Here is the kind of walkthrough we expect from any course worth its price. Question: a 20 foot run of 3/4 inch EMT will carry six 12 AWG THHN conductors. Is that a legal conduit fill? A weak course prints the answer (yes) and moves on. A good one walks the path: Chapter 9, Table 1 says more than two conductors means 40 percent fill. Chapter 9, Table 4 gives 3/4 EMT a 40 percent area of 0.213 square inches. Chapter 9, Table 5 gives 12 AWG THHN an area of 0.0133 square inches, so six conductors occupy 0.0798 square inches. Well under 0.213, legal, and Annex C would have gotten you there in one lookup.

That three-table dance is the whole exam in miniature. The same pattern shows up in box fill (314.16 counts, not guesses), ampacity derating (310.15 adjustment factors stacked on temperature correction), and every motor problem in Article 430. If a course demo lesson does not show the table path, assume the rest of the course does not either.

Red flags when you are comparing courses

  - No stated NEC edition anywhere on the sales page. That usually means recycled questions from two code cycles ago.

  - Guaranteed pass claims with fine print requiring you to complete 100 percent of modules before the guarantee applies.

  - Question banks under 300 questions. You will memorize answers instead of learning navigation by the second pass.

  - No timed mode. Untimed practice is homework, not exam prep.

  - Testimonials but no sample lesson. Every honest provider shows you a free module or demo exam.

None of these alone is disqualifying. Two or more, walk.

Exam-day tactics no course teaches well

Three passes, one clock. First pass: answer everything you know cold and every fast definition lookup, flag the rest. This banks 50 to 60 percent of the exam in the first hour. Second pass: the flagged code lookups, hardest chapter references last. Third pass: the long calculations, motors and services, with whatever time remains. People fail by burning 20 minutes on a motor problem in the first half hour, then rushing 30 easy lookups at the end.

Also, arrive with your book already broken in. Tabs on Articles 100, 210, 220, 250, 310, 314, 430, and Chapter 9 tables cover the majority of lookups on most state exams. We settled on that tab set after tracking which tables our own practice questions actually hit, and it has held up.

State wrinkles worth knowing

Texas runs one of the highest-volume journeyman exams in the country through PSI, and its blueprint leans hard on grounding and services. Other states swap in local amendments, and a few (looking at you, Washington) add rules from their own administrative codes that no national course covers. If your state amends the NEC, get the amendment list from the board site and drill it separately.

What no course can fix

A prep course polishes exam skills. It cannot supply the eligibility a state board wants underneath them: verified hours, apprenticeship completion or documented experience, application approval. If your paperwork is not in order, sort that first; a rejected application wastes the testing window your course prepared you for. And no course substitutes for field fluency. Candidates who have never landed a service or bent a saddle can pass a written exam, but the ones who struggle least are the ones for whom Table 310.16 describes wire they have actually pulled.

The other thing a course cannot do is take the exam calmly on your behalf. Exam anxiety is real in this trade, where plenty of excellent electricians have not sat a formal test in fifteen years. The only fix we have seen work is repetition: enough full-length timed exams that the real one is the eighth time you have done it, not the first. That costs nothing but evenings.

If you failed the exam: what to change before the retest

Failure diagnostics first, shopping second. Your score report breaks results down by content area, and it is the most useful document you own right now. Low across the board usually means a pacing problem, so the fix is timed full-length practice, not more content review. Low on calculations only means eight weeks focused on Articles 220, 310, and 430 with worked problems. Low on one or two code areas means targeted drilling, and a full course would waste your money on the 80 percent you already know.

Retest logistics vary: most states let you rebook after 24 hours to 30 days, and the fee (typically $75 to $150 through PSI) is due every attempt. A few states force remedial coursework after the third failure. Our position, having heard from a lot of retesters: the second attempt with a diagnosis and six weeks of targeted timed practice passes far more often than the first attempt did, so treat the first score report as tuition, not verdict.

FAQ

How long should I study for the journeyman electrician exam?

Plan on 60 to 100 total hours for most candidates, spread over 6 to 12 weeks. Electricians with broad field experience and strong math can compress that; anyone retesting after a failure should extend it and spend the extra time on timed code navigation, which is the most common failure point we hear about.

Are online prep courses as good as in-person classes?

For content, yes, and often better, because you can replay calculation walkthroughs. What online courses lack is enforced attendance. If you have skipped self-paced programs before, you will skip this one too, and an in-person class at a local trade school or supply house is worth the extra cost for the accountability alone.

Do prep courses include the NEC code book?

Usually not. The NEC 2026 (published by NFPA, https://www.nfpa.org) runs roughly $125 to $150 softbound and is a separate purchase. Buy the exact edition your state tests on, buy it early, and take your own tabbed copy to the exam if your state permits it. A borrowed, untabbed book on exam day is a real handicap.

What score do I need to pass?

Most states set the cut at 70 or 75 percent. On an 80-question exam, 70 percent means 56 correct, so you can miss 24 and still pass. That math should shape your strategy: flag the long motor calculations, bank the fast code lookups first, and come back with whatever time remains.

The journeyman exam rewards speed in the code book and fluency in a dozen calculations, and any prep worth paying for trains exactly those two things under a clock.
