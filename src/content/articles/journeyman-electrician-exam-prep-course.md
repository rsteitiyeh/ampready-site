A good journeyman electrician exam prep course matches your state's actual exam vendor and format, drills NEC code lookups under time pressure, and includes a full set of calculations practice (box fill, conduit fill, voltage drop, load calc). Skip courses built on an outdated NEC edition or ones that promise a guaranteed pass without giving you real timed practice tests.

Not every course is built the same way, and the differences matter more than the marketing copy suggests. Your state board sets the exam vendor, the NEC edition, the question count, and the time limit. A course written for a generic "national" exam can leave you unprepared for the specific format you'll sit for, so the first filter is always: does this course match my state's exam, not just the trade in general.

## Does the course match your state's exam vendor and format?

Check this before you pay for anything. States contract with different vendors (PSI, Pearson VUE, ICC, and others depending on the jurisdiction), and each vendor structures its exam differently: question count, time limit, and whether the test is open-book or closed-book with the NEC.

Texas is a useful example of why this matters. The Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation splits its journeyman exam into two separate PSI-administered parts, an NEC Knowledge section and a Calculations section, each requiring a 70% passing score, a structure TDLR moved to on March 11, 2025 according to its [electrician exam information page](https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/elecexam.htm). A course written for a single 80-question, four-hour exam won't prepare you for that split format, even if the underlying code content is the same. Confirm your state's current format directly with your licensing board before you commit to a course.

## Does it drill real NEC code navigation, or just flashcards?

The exam tests whether you can find the right code section fast, under a clock, in your own physical codebook. Flashcards test memorization of isolated facts. Those are different skills, and a course that only does the second one will leave you stuck when a question forces you to cross-reference three tables to get an answer.

Look for practice questions that require you to open the NEC and locate a specific article, table, or exception, the same way the real exam does. A course worth paying for teaches you where things live in the book (index, table of contents, article numbering logic) so you're not hunting blind on test day. Most state boards, including Texas TDLR, require candidates to bring their own soft-bound NEC codebook, with normal tabbing allowed but no loose-leaf pages or extra annotations, so practicing with your actual tabbed book matters more than practicing on a screen alone.

## Does it cover calculations, not just code lookups?

Calculations are usually the section that fails candidates, because they combine multiple code references into one problem. A prep course needs dedicated practice in at least these four areas:

- Box fill calculations (conductor count, device and clamp allowances, box volume)
- Conduit fill (percentage fill limits by conductor count, wire area from the code tables)
- Voltage drop (the standard formula using conductor resistance, distance, and load current)
- Service and feeder load calculations (general lighting load, demand factors, standard vs. optional method)

If a course's sample material is all multiple-choice code trivia with no worked math problems, it's not going to prepare you for the calculations portion of your exam, whether that's a standalone section like Texas now runs or folded into a mixed-format test in other states.

## Do the practice tests match real question counts and time limits?

A practice test that gives you 20 easy questions with no clock does nothing for exam-day pacing. You need practice tests that mimic the actual number of questions and time allowed so you learn how to budget minutes per question and when to flag and move on. Pacing is a skill you build through repetition under realistic constraints, not something you pick up by reading strategy tips.

## Is the course built on the current NEC edition your state tests?

This is where a lot of otherwise decent courses fail candidates. The National Fire Protection Association published the 2026 edition of the NEC on August 20, 2025, but most state boards will not require it for licensing exams until sometime between 2026 and 2028, according to [NFPA's own NEC product page](https://www.nfpa.org/product/nfpa-70-national-electrical-code-nec/p0070code). As of mid-2026, a large share of states still test candidates on the 2020 NEC rather than the 2023 edition, based on the code-adoption tracker maintained by the [International Association of Electrical Inspectors](https://www.iaei.org/page/nec-code-adoption). Buying a course tied to the wrong edition means memorizing article numbers and table values that don't match what's in front of you on test day. Confirm which edition your board tests before you buy anything, and cross-check it against the course's stated edition.

## What to look for vs. what to skip

| Look for | Skip |
|---|---|
| States which exam vendor and NEC edition it targets | Vague "prep for any state" marketing with no vendor or edition named |
| Timed practice tests matching your state's real question count | Untimed quizzes with no pacing practice |
| Dedicated calculations modules (box fill, conduit fill, voltage drop, load calc) | Code trivia only, no worked math problems |
| Code-navigation drills using your actual codebook | Flashcard-only memorization with no codebook practice |
| Current NEC edition matching your state's adopted edition | Outdated edition with no update noted |
| Honest pass-rate framing tied to practice volume | "Pass guaranteed" claims with no real practice included |

## What "pass guaranteed" claims usually mean

Be skeptical of any course that promises a guaranteed pass. No prep course controls your state board's exam or grading. What "guarantee" usually means in the fine print is a partial refund or extra access time, not an actual pass. The better signal is practice volume: how many full-length, timed practice tests does the course include, and do they cover both code lookups and calculations in the same proportions as your real exam.

## FAQ

**Do I need a prep course if I already have on-the-job experience?**
Field experience teaches you to do the work, not necessarily to navigate the codebook fast under exam conditions. Most candidates benefit from focused calculations and timed code-lookup practice even after years on the job, since the exam format itself is a separate skill from daily fieldwork.

**How long should I study before sitting for the journeyman exam?**
Most candidates spend 4 to 8 weeks on focused review, longer if calculations are a weak area. Base your timeline on how many full-length practice tests you can realistically complete, not a fixed calendar number.

**Can I use a prep course written for a different state?**
Only for general code-navigation and calculations skills. The vendor, format, question count, and NEC edition are state-specific, so cross-check any out-of-state course against your own board's current bulletin before relying on it for format expectations.

**Does an open-book exam mean I don't need to memorize anything?**
No. Open-book exams like Texas's still require you to know where information lives in the NEC and how to apply it quickly. Candidates who haven't practiced navigation often run out of time even with the book in hand.

**What's the difference between a code-lookup question and a calculation question?**
A code-lookup question asks you to find and apply a specific rule (like GFCI requirements or receptacle spacing). A calculation question asks you to compute a number (like conduit fill percentage or voltage drop) using values from multiple code tables. A solid course drills both separately.
