# Cost to Become a Journeyman Electrician: Full Breakdown

Most people spend $450 to $2,500 out of pocket to become a journeyman electrician if they go the standard paid apprenticeship route, mostly tools, a code book, and license fees. Add trade school first and the total climbs to $4,000-$18,000. The apprenticeship itself pays you; it is not a bill.

That gap surprises people who assume a licensed trade costs as much as a two-year degree. It doesn't, if you take the apprenticeship path. The real cost swings on one choice: learn on a paid job site, or pay a school first.

## What does a paid apprenticeship actually cost?

Close to nothing in tuition, because a registered apprenticeship is a job, not a school. You work under a licensed electrician, earn a percentage of journeyman wage from day one, and your classroom hours are usually covered by the union training fund or your employer.

Registered electrician apprenticeships run about four years and require roughly 8,000 hours of on-the-job training plus 576 to 900 hours of classroom instruction, depending on the program, according to the U.S. Department of Labor's [Apprenticeship.gov occupation listing](https://www.apprenticeship.gov/apprenticeship-occupations/listings?occupationCode=47-2111.00). Union (IBEW/JATC) programs typically charge no tuition. Non-union or merit-shop programs sometimes bill $500 to $2,000 a year in classroom fees, so ask before you sign up.

Because you're earning during those four years, most apprentices come out ahead financially compared to someone paying trade-school tuition while working part time or not at all.

## What if you go to trade school first?

You'll pay tuition, and it can still shorten your path to a supervised job. A community college electrical program typically costs $3,500 to $8,000 for six months to two years of training, and a private trade school runs closer to $14,000 to $16,000 for a similar timeline, per [BestColleges' 2026 trade school cost research](https://www.bestcolleges.com/research/how-much-does-trade-school-cost/).

Trade school is not required to become a journeyman electrician anywhere in the US; it's an optional head start. Some people use it to land a better apprenticeship placement or to bank a few hundred classroom hours early. Others go straight into an apprenticeship and skip tuition entirely.

## How much are the exam and license fees?

Not much, relative to the rest of the process, usually $90 to $150 combined. We checked the current fee schedule directly against Texas's licensing board rather than relying on a secondhand roundup. Texas requires 7,000 hours of on-the-job training before you can sit the journeyman exam, and 8,000 hours total for licensure, according to the [Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation](https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/apply/individuals/journeyman-electrician.htm). The Texas journeyman application carries a non-refundable $30 fee, and the PSI-administered exam runs about $78 as of the current [TDLR Candidate Information Bulletin](https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/pdf/Candidate-Information-Bulletin.pdf), which moved to a two-part knowledge-and-calculations format in March 2025.

Other states follow a similar pattern: a $30-$45 state application fee plus a $60-$100 exam fee, usually paid straight to the testing vendor (PSI or Pearson VUE). Fees and exam structure vary by state, so confirm current numbers with your own licensing board before you schedule anything.

## What do tools and PPE cost?

Expect to spend $300 to $2,000 building out a basic kit, more if your employer doesn't supply anything. A first-year apprentice usually needs hand tools (linesman pliers, strippers, a good screwdriver set), a multimeter, work boots, and basic PPE. Many contractors and union shops supply the big-ticket items, like conduit benders and ladders, and just expect you to own hand tools. A current National Electrical Code book and a practice-exam study guide run another $60 to $150; AmpExam's own study guide is built around exactly that gap for people prepping for the exam itself.

Trade school makes more sense if you need structured classroom time you can't get through a union hall or employer, or you're changing careers and want a faster on-ramp into a program.

## Does master electrician cost more?

Yes, but not until years later. In Texas, you need two years as a licensed journeyman plus 12,000 total hours of on-the-job training to apply for a master electrician license, and the application fee jumps to $45, per [TDLR's master electrician requirements](https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/apply/individuals/master-electrician.htm). The added cost at that stage is mostly the exam fee and study time, not a new round of tuition. Most people who reach master status are already working full time as journeymen, so the added cost is small compared to what they spent getting licensed in the first place.

## FAQ

**How much does the journeyman electrician exam cost?**
Exam fees typically run $60 to $100, paid to the testing vendor, not the state. Texas charges about $78 through PSI. That's separate from the state application fee, which is usually $30 to $45. Always confirm current pricing with your state licensing board since testing vendors update fees periodically.

**Do apprentice electricians get paid while training?**
Yes. Registered apprentices earn a percentage of full journeyman wage, usually starting around 40-50% and stepping up each year as hours accumulate. This is the biggest cost difference between an apprenticeship and trade school: one pays you, the other bills you.

**Is trade school required to become a journeyman electrician?**
No. Every state licenses journeyman electricians through a documented hours-and-exam path, not a degree requirement. Trade school is one way to get classroom hours and basic skills before you find an apprenticeship, but a paid apprenticeship alone satisfies the requirement in every state.

**How much do tools and PPE cost for a new electrician?**
Budget $300 to $2,000 for a starter kit of hand tools, a multimeter, boots, and basic safety gear. Most contractors and union shops supply larger equipment like benders and ladders. Costs spread out over your first year rather than hitting all at once.

**Does the cost to become a journeyman electrician vary by state?**
Yes, mainly through application and exam fees, which run roughly $90-$150 combined nationally. Required on-the-job hours are close to the national 8,000-hour standard in most states, though a few set their own thresholds, so check your state licensing board directly.
