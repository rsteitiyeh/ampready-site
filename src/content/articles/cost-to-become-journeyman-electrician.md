Plan on roughly $600 to $1,500 all-in to become a journeyman electrician through an apprenticeship, spread over four to five years. That covers application fees, tools, the codebook, exam and license fees, and prep materials. You earn wages the whole time. Trade school instead of (or before) an apprenticeship adds $5,000 to $20,000.

The reason the range is that wide, and that low, is the apprenticeship model. Unlike degrees that front-load debt, the standard path pays you from week one while the licensing costs trickle out in small chunks. Below is every line item, what drives it up or down, and where the numbers come from. Fees are current as of mid-2026 to the best of our checking; state boards change them, so verify yours.

Apprenticeship: the earn-while-you-learn math

The four-to-five-year apprenticeship is where the economics of this trade beat almost every licensed profession. Application fees run $25 to $100 for most JATC and union programs, and plenty of open-shop programs charge nothing beyond a drug screen. From there you work paid hours, typically starting around half of journeyman scale and stepping up each year.

Set that against the wage on the other side. The Bureau of Labor Statistics puts the 2024 median wage for electricians at roughly $62,350 per year (https://www.bls.gov/ooh/construction-and-extraction/electricians.htm, approximate). A licensing path that costs about what one nice hand tool set costs, and ends at a median north of sixty grand, is a rare deal. Our overview of pay by state and specialty is at AmpExam/how-much-do-journeyman-electricians-make.

Some union programs also deduct small book or dues amounts from checks during the apprenticeship. Ask up front; it is usually modest but it is real money.

Trade school: the expensive optional detour

Trade school is the one item that can multiply your total by ten. Programs run $5,000 to $20,000 for 6 to 24 months, and here is the part schools do not advertise: most states credit classroom hours only partially toward the on-the-job hours a journeyman license requires. You may still owe most of the same apprenticeship time afterward.

When it makes sense: you cannot land an apprenticeship slot in your area, your state grants meaningful hour credit for classroom time, or an employer is paying. Otherwise, apply to programs first. Tools: $300 to $800, built gradually

Most contractors hand new apprentices a required tool list. A workable starter kit lands between $300 and $800:

  - Hand tools: linesman pliers, strippers, channel locks, screwdrivers, level ($150 to $350)

  - Multimeter and voltage tester ($60 to $200)

  - Tool bag or pouch and belt ($40 to $120)

  - Work boots, EH rated ($100 to $200)

Buy the pliers and strippers in the good brands and go mid-range on everything else. Nobody's first meter needs to be the $400 one. Power tools are almost always supplied by the contractor at this stage.

Exam and license fees, with a real number

Three separate fees hit near the end of the path, and candidates routinely conflate them. The exam application fee ($30 to $75) goes to your state board to approve you to test. The exam fee itself ($50 to $100) goes to the testing vendor. Texas is our verified example: the TDLR journeyman exam fee is $78 (check current), with details at https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/electricians.htm. Then the license issuance fee runs $30 to $120 depending on the state.

Budget for one retake. Pass rates on first attempts hover in the 60 to 75 percent range in the states that publish them, and a retake usually means paying the exam fee again. The cheapest insurance against that is prep, which brings us to the next line.

Codebook and prep materials

You need the National Electrical Code, full stop. Most journeyman exams are open book, so the codebook is both your study text and your exam-day reference. NFPA publishes it (https://www.nfpa.org) and a new bound edition runs about $125 to $200 depending on format and tabbing. Buy the edition your state has adopted, which is not always the newest cycle, and buy bound, since spiral and loose-leaf copies fail materials checks in some states. Whether your state lets you bring it in, and how to tab it, is covered in our open-book rules article at AmpExam/is-the-journeyman-electrician-test-open-book.

Prep is where budgets diverge. Full prep courses run $100 to $500. They earn their price for candidates who need structure and calculation walkthroughs on conduit fill, box fill, and ampacity derating. Renewal: the cost that never stops

The journeyman license renews every one to three years depending on the state, at $30 to $100 per cycle, and many states require continuing education hours on top, often four to eight hours covering code updates. CE courses typically run $20 to $100. It is a small line item, but it is permanent, so put it in the budget rather than getting surprised by a lapsed-license reinstatement fee, which usually costs more than renewing on time would have.

What we would actually spend

If we were starting today with an apprenticeship slot in hand: about $60 on the application, $450 on tools spread over year one, $160 on the codebook in the year we test, $19 on the study guide, roughly $200 in combined exam and license fees. Call it $900, spread across four years of paychecks. We ran this list past two journeymen who tested in the last three years and the only pushback was that one of them spent more on boots.

FAQ

Is becoming an electrician cheaper than college?

Dramatically. The apprenticeship path costs roughly $600 to $1,500 total over four to five years, during which you earn wages that rise each year. A four-year degree averages tens of thousands in tuition with no wages attached. The trade-off is physical work and a licensing exam, not debt. Trade school narrows the gap but stays far below university cost.

How much is the journeyman electrician exam fee?

Most states charge $50 to $100 for the exam itself, paid to the testing vendor, plus a separate $30 to $75 application fee to the state board. Texas TDLR lists its journeyman exam fee at $78 (check current) at https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/electricians.htm. Failing means paying the exam fee again for a retake, so prep is cheaper than retesting.

Do apprentices pay for their own tools?

Usually yes for hand tools, meter, and boots, which is the $300 to $800 line in our table. Contractors normally supply power tools, ladders, and consumables. Most shops hand out a required tool list on day one and expect it filled within a few weeks, though plenty of apprentices spread purchases over the first several months as checks come in.

Can I skip trade school and still get licensed?

In every state we have checked, yes. Licensing is based on documented on-the-job hours (commonly around 8,000) plus classroom-related training that apprenticeship programs provide as part of the deal. Trade school is a paid alternative for the classroom portion, not a requirement. Apply to apprenticeship programs first and treat tuition as the fallback, not the default.

How long does it take to earn back the cost?

Almost immediately, because you were earning the whole time. Even at first-year apprentice wages, the entire $600 to $1,500 licensing cost is a week or two of gross pay. At the 2024 median electrician wage of roughly $62,350 per year (https://www.bls.gov/ooh/construction-and-extraction/electricians.htm, approximate), the payback question basically answers itself.

Bottom line

Becoming a journeyman electrician costs about $600 to $1,500 through an apprenticeship, and you draw a paycheck for every year of it. Trade school can add $5,000 to $20,000 and is optional in every state we have checked. The two spends that most affect your outcome are the tabbed NEC codebook and honest exam prep, because a failed exam costs a retake fee and months of waiting.
