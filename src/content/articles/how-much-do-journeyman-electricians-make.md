**Journeyman electricians in the US earn roughly $60,000 to $80,000 a year in most markets. BLS 2024 data puts the median annual wage for all electricians at about $62,350, with the top 10 percent above roughly $104,000 and the bottom 10 percent below about $40,000. Location, union status, and overtime move the number more than anything else.**

That is the short version. The longer version depends on where you swing your Kleins, who signs your check, and how many Saturdays you are willing to work. We pulled the numbers below from the Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics program (occupation code 47-2111) and its Occupational Outlook Handbook, and we labeled everything as approximate 2024 data because wages drift every survey cycle.

The national numbers, from BLS

BLS tracks electricians as one occupation, so the published figures blend apprentices, journeymen, and some working masters. That blending matters. A licensed journeyman with a few years on the card usually sits above the published median, because the bottom of the BLS distribution is heavy with first and second year apprentices.

The 2024 figures from the Occupational Outlook Handbook (https://www.bls.gov/ooh/construction-and-extraction/electricians.htm) and the OEWS table for occupation 47-2111 (https://www.bls.gov/oes/current/oes472111.htm), all approximate:

BLS also projects about 9 percent employment growth for electricians from 2024 to 2034, faster than the average across all occupations. Data centers, EV infrastructure, and grid work are pulling demand, and a lot of the boomer-generation workforce is aging out. Both trends favor wages.

Illinois, Washington, and New York run high because of union density and prevailing-wage work. Texas and Florida run below the national median, but so does their cost of living, and neither taxes wage income. A $56,000 Texas paycheck can spend like a $75,000 check in a coastal metro. We always tell readers to compare wage against local rent before chasing a number on a map.

One honest caveat: statewide medians hide huge metro spreads. An electrician in San Francisco and one in Bakersfield both count toward the California figure, and their checks do not look alike.

Union vs non-union pay

Union scale is public, which makes it the easiest wage data in the trade to verify. IBEW journeyman inside-wireman scale in major metros commonly runs $45 to $60 an hour on the check, plus a benefits package (pension, annuity, health) that can add another 50 to 70 percent on top. Non-union shops in the same markets typically pay $28 to $45 an hour, sometimes with a match and health, sometimes with a handshake.

The trade-off is real, though. Union work can mean the bench between calls. Merit-shop guys often get steadier 40s plus a truck. Over a full year the gap narrows more than the hourly rates suggest, unless the union hand is catching overtime, which brings us to the next section.

Overtime and per diem, where the real money hides

Nobody clears six figures on straight 40s at median scale. The journeymen we know who post big years do it three ways:

  - Overtime. Time and a half after 40 (double time on some agreements). A journeyman at $40 an hour working 50-hour weeks grosses about $114,000 a year. Same hand at straight 40s grosses $83,200.

  - Per diem. Traveling industrial and data-center work commonly pays $80 to $150 a day untaxed on top of wages. Forty weeks on the road at $100 a day is $20,000 that never sees withholding.

  - Shutdowns and storm work. Refinery turnarounds and storm restoration pay stacked overtime for weeks at a stretch. Brutal hours, memorable checks.

We ran that 50-hour math three different ways before publishing because the first pass looked too clean. It held. Overtime is the single biggest lever a journeyman controls.

How wage type changes the picture

Residential, commercial, and industrial are almost three different pay scales wearing one job title. Resi service work tends to sit at the bottom of the range, though commission-heavy service shops can surprise you; a good service electrician selling panel swaps in a wealthy suburb can out-earn a commercial hand. Commercial construction is the broad middle. Industrial, controls, and data-center work anchor the top, and those jobs also come with the most overtime and travel per diem attached.

Government and utility jobs deserve a mention too. Municipal and utility electricians often trade a few dollars of hourly rate for pensions, stability, and 40-hour weeks that actually stay 40. Over a career, that pension math can beat a higher hourly rate at a shop that lays off every winter. We know hands on both sides of that trade who defend their choice loudly.

Apprentice vs journeyman vs master pay

The license ladder is also a pay ladder. Typical relationships we see across markets, expressed against local journeyman scale:

  - Apprentices start around 40 to 50 percent of journeyman scale and step up every 1,000 hours or every year, reaching 80 to 90 percent by the final period.

  - Journeymen set the local scale. That is the number in the chart above.

  - Masters typically earn 15 to 30 percent over journeyman scale as employees, and considerably more running their own shop, along with the liability that comes with pulling permits.

What the averages hide

Two things the tidy tables above do not show. First, layoffs. Construction is cyclical, and a journeyman who banks $85,000 in a boom year can sit at 60 percent of that when the local market cools. Averaging your last three W-2s tells you more than any single year. Second, the benefits gap. Two $38-an-hour offers are not equal if one includes a funded pension and family health coverage and the other includes a gas card. When we compare offers for readers, we price the benefits package at 30 to 60 percent of wages, because that is roughly what it costs to replace out of pocket.

FAQ

How much does a journeyman electrician make an hour?

Divide the annual figures by 2,080 hours. The approximate 2024 BLS median of $62,350 works out to about $30 an hour across all electricians. Licensed journeymen usually land higher, roughly $28 to $45 an hour non-union and $45 to $60 on union scale in strong metros. Overtime at time and a half changes the effective rate fast.

Do journeyman electricians make six figures?

Some do. BLS shows the top 10 percent of electricians above roughly $104,000 in 2024. Getting there almost always means one of three things: union scale in a high-wage metro, heavy overtime on industrial or data-center work, or per diem travel jobs. Straight 40s at median scale will not get you there on wages alone.

Is being a journeyman electrician worth it financially?

The math favors it. Median pay near $62,350, no student debt because apprentices earn while they train, and BLS projects about 9 percent growth from 2024 to 2034. Compare that against a four-year degree that costs money instead of paying it. The trade is hard on the body, which is the honest cost nobody puts in a wage table.

What state pays journeyman electricians the most?

Illinois, Washington, New York, Oregon, and Hawaii consistently top the OEWS tables, with approximate 2024 medians in the $80,000 to $92,000 range. High union density and prevailing-wage laws drive most of it. Adjust for cost of living before relocating; a lower nominal wage in Texas or Florida can leave more in your pocket.

Bottom line

Expect roughly $62,000 at the national median, $80,000 or more as an experienced journeyman in a strong market, and six figures if you stack union scale, overtime, or travel per diem. All figures are approximate 2024 BLS data and will move. The one raise fully inside your control is the license itself.
