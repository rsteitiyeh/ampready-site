The Texas journeyman electrician exam is administered for the Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation (TDLR) and delivered through PSI. It is a 4-hour, open-book, NEC-based exam with a passing score of 70%. The exam fee is $78 (check current amount). Practice questions that mirror that format are below.

Texas does not write its own separate electrical code for the exam; the questions come from the NEC (current edition NEC 2026, published by NFPA, https://www.nfpa.org) plus the Texas Electrical Safety and Licensing Act and TDLR rules. Requirements, fees, and scheduling details live on the TDLR page at https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/electricians.htm, and that page controls if anything here has changed.

Two details trip people up. First, open book means the permitted NEC edition, and PSI's rules control what tabs and notes are allowed, so read the candidate information bulletin before test day. Second, the 70% pass mark sounds forgiving until you factor time: 4 hours across the full question set leaves only a few minutes per question, including code lookups.

6 sample questions in the TDLR exam style

These are our own questions, written to match the lookup-heavy style of the Texas exam. Work them open book against NEC 2026.

Question 1: receptacle spacing

In a dwelling, no point along the wall line of a habitable room may be farther than how many feet from a receptacle?

Answer: 6 ft, per 210.52(A). That produces the familiar 12 ft maximum spacing between receptacles. Any wall space 2 ft or wider needs one.

Question 2: GFCI protection

Which dwelling locations require GFCI protection for 125 V, 15 and 20 A receptacles?

Answer: 210.8(A) lists them: bathrooms, garages, outdoors, crawl spaces, basements, kitchens, sink areas within 6 ft, boathouses, laundry areas, and similar locations. On the exam, scan the answer choices against the printed list rather than trusting memory; the list has grown with each code cycle.

Question 3: burial depth

What is the minimum cover for UF cable serving a 120 V, 20 A, GFCI-protected residential branch circuit, direct buried under a lawn?

Answer: Table 300.5 generally requires 24 in for direct-buried cable, but the residential exception for 120 V, 20 A or less, GFCI-protected circuits permits 12 in. That exception column is a favorite exam trap.

Question 4: working clearance

What is the minimum depth of working space in front of a 240 V panelboard, Condition 1?

Answer: 3 ft, per Table 110.26(A)(1). Depth runs 3 ft to 4 ft as voltage and conditions increase, and the width is 30 in or the equipment width, whichever is greater, with headroom of 6.5 ft.

Question 5: water heater calculation

A 4,500 W, 240 V storage water heater. What is the minimum branch-circuit conductor ampacity, and what breaker fits?

Answer: Current = 4,500 / 240 = 18.75 A. Article 422 treats a fixed storage water heater as a continuous load, so 18.75 x 1.25 = 23.44 A minimum. 10 AWG copper at 30 A covers it, protected at 25 A or 30 A. We double-checked the arithmetic: 18.75 x 1.25 is exactly 23.4375, so a 20 A circuit fails and a 25 A circuit passes.

Question 6: conductor identification

Which colors are reserved for grounded conductors?

Answer: White or gray, per 200.6. Green or green with yellow stripes is reserved for equipment grounding conductors, and an exam question will often bait you with green as a 'grounded' rather than 'grounding' answer. Read which word the question uses.

Test day logistics with PSI

PSI runs test centers across Texas, with sites in and around Houston, Dallas, Fort Worth, Austin, San Antonio, El Paso, and several smaller markets. You book after TDLR approves your application and PSI issues an eligibility notice. Bring government-issued photo ID that matches your application name exactly; a mismatch can void the appointment and the fee.

Your code book gets inspected. Permanent tabs are generally allowed while loose notes are not, but the candidate bulletin is the controlling document and its rules have changed over the years, so read the current version the week before your date rather than trusting a coworker's memory from 2022. Arrive 30 minutes early; late arrivals forfeit the seat. Phones and smartwatches stay in a locker.

Results come at the center the same day. A failing report lists your score by content area, which turns the retake from a guess into a targeted drill list. We tell candidates to schedule the retake within 3 to 4 weeks while the material is warm, and to spend that window almost entirely on the two weakest content areas from the report.

Common mistakes Texas candidates make

  - Studying general electrical theory when the exam is mostly NEC navigation. The code book, not the textbook, is the exam.

  - Bringing the wrong NEC edition. Confirm the edition in the current PSI bulletin before you buy tabs.

  - Skipping the Texas licensing law questions. They are a small block, but free points if you have read the act once.

  - Burning 10 minutes on one calculation early. Flag it, move, and return with pooled time.

  - Booking the exam before TDLR confirms hours. The application review comes first, and processing takes time.

FAQ

Is the Texas journeyman electrician exam open book?

Yes. The TDLR journeyman exam delivered through PSI is open book with the NEC. PSI's candidate bulletin governs which edition and what tabbing is permitted, so verify before test day. Open book rewards navigation speed, not memorization; practice finding articles fast. More on open-book strategy at AmpExam/is-the-journeyman-electrician-test-open-book.

How much does the Texas journeyman exam cost?

The exam fee is $78 (check current amount), paid to the test vendor, and TDLR charges separate application and license fees. Fees change, so confirm the current schedule at https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/electricians.htm before budgeting. Retakes mean paying the exam fee again, which is a good reason to over-prepare.

What score do I need to pass in Texas?

70%. You will typically see your result at the PSI test center the same day. If you fail, you can reapply and retest per TDLR's rules, paying the exam fee each attempt. The score report shows performance by content area, which tells you exactly what to drill before the retake.

How many hours do I need before I can test?

Texas requires 8,000 hours of on-the-job training under the supervision of a master electrician for the journeyman license. Verify your logged hours through your employer and confirm current requirements on the TDLR electricians page, since documentation rules occasionally change. Apprentices building hours can start with our material at AmpExam/apprentice-electrician-training.
