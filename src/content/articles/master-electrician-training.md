Master electrician training is the stage after your journeyman license: typically 2 more years (about 4,000 hours) of documented experience as a licensed journeyman, followed by a longer, harder master exam covering NEC calculations, design, and contracting rules. Some states want 1 year, some 4. There is no national license; your state board sets the bar.

We keep this page keyed to patterns rather than a 50-state table because boards change fees and hour rules constantly. Every figure below is approximate and worth ten minutes of verification on your own board's site before you plan around it.

Where the master license fits

The journeyman license says you can install to code under a contractor's umbrella. The master license says you can be the umbrella: design systems, pull permits, supervise journeymen and apprentices, and in most states qualify an electrical contracting business. That last part is the economic point. The master is usually the license attached to the company, the insurance, and the profit margin, which is why it is the ceiling credential in states that use the apprentice, journeyman, master ladder.

Typical state requirement patterns

State rules cluster into a few recognizable shapes. The most common pattern is 2 years, roughly 4,000 hours, as a licensed journeyman, then the master exam. Texas is the textbook case: 12,000 total hours including 2 years holding the journeyman license. Washington sits at the strict end, requiring 4 years as a journeyman before the master application. A handful of states (Minnesota, Virginia, and Colorado among them) accept about 1 year of journeyman tenure, usually with larger total-hour thresholds underneath.

Other recurring patterns worth knowing: several states accept an electrical engineering degree plus reduced field hours; a few have no state master license at all and leave it to cities or counties; and reciprocity between states is spotty at the master level even where journeyman reciprocity exists. If a move is in your future, check both boards before you schedule the exam.

What the master exam adds

The master exam is the journeyman exam with the difficulty knob turned up and a business layer bolted on. Same open-book format in most states, same vendor (often PSI), but longer, with a heavier calculation load and more design-oriented questions. Expect deeper coverage of service and feeder calculations, transformer sizing and connections, fault current basics, motor circuit design end to end, and multi-family load calculations that take three tables to finish. The reference is the NEC, currently the 2026 edition published by NFPA (https://www.nfpa.org), though several boards still test on 2023. Confirm the edition on your candidate bulletin before you tab a book.

Many states also fold in contractor-adjacent content: grounding electrode design, pool and special-occupancy rules, and state administrative law. A few split this into a separate business and law exam with its own fee and booking.

A realistic training plan for the journeyman years

The smartest thing we did in our own journeyman years, and what we hear from masters who passed first try, is treating those 4,000 hours as training rather than waiting. The exam rewards breadth you can steer toward on the job.

1.  Year 1, first half: chase service and feeder work. Volunteer for panel changes and service upgrades; the exam's load calculations map directly onto that work.

2.  Year 1, second half: get onto motor and control work if you can. Article 430 is the heaviest calculation cluster on most master exams.

3.  Year 2, first half: start formal study, 3 to 5 hours a week. Work every calculation type on paper: ampacity with adjustment factors, conduit fill, box fill, transformers, voltage drop.

4.  Year 2, second half: shift to timed full-length practice exams weekly, review every miss against the code text, and book the exam for the month you clear the hour requirement.

What master-level calculation questions look like

A concrete example, because "harder calculations" is vague. A typical master exam problem: size the service conductors for a 12-unit multi-family dwelling where each unit has 900 square feet, an 8 kW range, and electric dryers. Solving it means the general lighting load at 3 VA per square foot from Table 220.42, the range demand factors from Table 220.55 column C, dryer demands from 220.54, then the optional method comparison in 220.84 to see if it produces a smaller service. That is four table lookups, a demand-factor judgment call, and arithmetic, in maybe six minutes of budgeted time. The journeyman exam asks pieces of that; the master exam asks the whole thing, repeatedly.

Transformer problems get the same treatment: primary and secondary protection per 450.3, conductor sizing on both sides, and grounding electrode conductor sizing from 250.66. If you cannot run a delta-wye problem end to end on paper right now, that is your study list, and it is a very fixable gap in six months of evenings.

Keeping the license once you have it

Budget for continuing education from day one. Most states require 8 to 16 hours of CEUs per renewal cycle, usually tied to the code update, and renewal fees run $30 to $500 depending on the state and whether the license also qualifies a contracting business. Some states also require masters who qualify a business to carry a bond or minimum insurance, which is a real annual cost worth pricing before you commit to the contractor path. None of this is hard; all of it is easy to miss, and a lapsed master license can mean re-examination in the less forgiving states.

Mistakes we see repeatedly

  - Applying before the hours are verifiable. Boards want signed verification, W-2s, or license history, and a rejected application can cost you a testing window.

  - Studying journeyman-depth material. The master exam assumes journeyman fluency and tests past it; old prep materials give false confidence.

  - Ignoring the business and law exam in states that require one. It is easy material, but a separate booking and a separate fee, and people fail it cold.

  - Tabbing a borrowed code book the night before. Your tabs are your speed. Build them over months, in the edition your state tests.

Moving states with a master license

Reciprocity at the master level is thinner than journeyman reciprocity, and the two are frequently confused. A state that honors your journeyman card may still require its own master exam, its own tenure verification, or both. The workable pattern when relocating: get your current board to issue a license verification letter, gather your original hour documentation (boards do not forward it), and check whether the destination state credits out-of-state journeyman tenure toward its master requirement. Some do in full, some at a discount, and a few start your clock over. Ten minutes on two board websites before the moving truck is booked can save a year of re-qualifying.

FAQ

How long after journeyman can I take the master exam?

Most states require 1 to 2 years as a licensed journeyman, with 2 years (about 4,000 hours) the most common pattern. Texas requires 2 years within 12,000 total hours; Washington requires 4 years. Check your board's current rule directly, because several states have adjusted tenure requirements over the last few code cycles.

Is the master electrician exam harder than the journeyman exam?

Yes, mainly in calculation depth and time pressure. Expect longer service, feeder, transformer, and motor problems, plus design questions the journeyman exam never asks. Pass rates run lower, and most candidates who fail tell us they ran out of time on the calculation-heavy back half rather than hitting unfamiliar topics.

Do I need a master license to start an electrical business?

In most states, effectively yes: the contractor license must be qualified by a master electrician, either you or someone you employ. A few states separate the contractor credential from the master license, and some handle licensing at the city level. Your state board's contractor page will say which model applies to you.

Can I train for the master exam while working full time?

That is how nearly everyone does it. Six months at 3 to 5 hours a week is a workable runway if your calculations are solid; closer to nine months if they are rusty. The working hours themselves count as preparation when you steer toward services, motors, and design tasks instead of repetitive rough-in.

Bottom line

Master electrician training is mostly deliberate use of the 1 to 4 years your state makes you wait: steer your field hours toward services and motors, verify your board's exact tenure rule and NEC edition, and spend the final six months on timed calculation practice.
