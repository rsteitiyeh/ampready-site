It depends on your state, but in most of them, yes. The majority of journeyman electrician exams, including the PSI-administered exam in Texas, are open book: you bring your own NEC codebook into the testing room. A handful of states run closed-book exams or supply a reference on screen instead. Always confirm with your state board before test day.

That answer covers the headline question, but the details matter more than the yes or no. Which edition of the code you can bring, whether your tabs and highlights are legal, and whether notes in the margins get your book confiscated all vary by state and by testing vendor. We have watched candidates lose their codebook at check-in over a single stapled insert, so read your candidate bulletin twice.

Why the answer varies by state

Electrical licensing is state law, not federal, so every state board sets its own exam rules. Most contract the exam out to a testing vendor, and PSI runs the largest share of journeyman exams in the US. Even among PSI states the reference rules differ, because the vendor enforces whatever the board specifies.

Texas is a clean example of the open-book model. The Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation (TDLR) administers its journeyman exam through PSI and allows the National Electrical Code as a reference in the room. TDLR publishes the current rules, fees, and candidate information at https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/electricians.htm, and that page is the one to trust over any forum thread.

Other states go the opposite way. Some run fully closed-book exams that lean harder on memorized code values. A third group provides the reference for you, either as an on-screen searchable PDF at the testing center or as a loaner codebook at the desk, so your personal tabbed copy stays in the car.

Tabbing and highlighting rules

Tabs are legal in most open-book states, and they are the single highest-leverage prep move you can make. The common restrictions:

  - Tabs must be permanently affixed. Sticky notes and paper clips are the classic check-in failure.

  - Tabs can be labeled (Article 250, Table 310.16, conduit fill tables) but usually cannot carry formulas or answers.

  - Highlighting and underlining are generally fine. Writing worked solutions in the margins usually is not.

  - Some centers flip through every page of your codebook before seating you. Budget ten extra minutes.

The NEC itself is published by the National Fire Protection Association, and pre-tabbed editions are sold through https://www.nfpa.org and most trade suppliers. If you buy a pre-tabbed copy, still confirm the tab style is legal in your state. We have seen one brand of plastic tab rejected at a center that accepted another.

How open book changes your study strategy

Candidates hear open book and relax. That is the trap. The exam is timed, typically around four hours for 60 to 100 questions, and the codebook is nearly 1,000 pages. If you need three minutes to find the ampacity table, you will run out of clock long before you run out of questions.

So the skill being tested is navigation speed, not recall. Our approach:

  - Learn the code's structure cold: chapters 1 through 4 apply generally, 5 through 7 are special occupancies and equipment, chapter 9 holds the tables.

  - Drill the twenty or so most-used spots until you can open within a few pages of them: 240.6 standard breaker sizes, Table 310.16 ampacity, 314.16 box fill, Chapter 9 conduit fill, Article 430 motors.

  - Practice every question with the physical codebook in hand and a timer running. Screen PDFs train the wrong muscle memory.

  - Memorize only what is faster than looking up: the ampacity of common conductors, standard breaker sizes, basic Ohm's law and power formulas.

FAQ

Can I bring my own NEC codebook to the journeyman exam?

In most open-book states, yes, and you must: testing centers generally do not lend codebooks in bring-your-own states. It has to be a bound copy of the edition your state has adopted, with no loose pages or inserts. Check your candidate bulletin for the exact edition, because several states still test on an older code cycle.

Are tabs allowed in the NEC during the test?

Usually yes, if they are permanently attached. Sticky notes are the most common item confiscated at check-in. Labels naming an article or table are typically fine; tabs carrying formulas or worked answers are typically not. Pre-tabbed codebooks sold through NFPA and trade suppliers meet most states' rules, but verify the tab style against your own state's bulletin.

Is the Texas journeyman electrician exam open book?

Yes. Texas administers its journeyman exam through PSI and allows the NEC as a reference in the testing room, subject to PSI's materials rules. TDLR publishes current requirements, fees, and the candidate bulletin at https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/electricians/electricians.htm, and that page reflects rule changes faster than any third-party site, including ours.

Does open book mean the test is easy?

No. The time limit does the filtering. With roughly two to four minutes per question and a nearly 1,000-page reference, candidates who never drilled timed lookups fail open-book exams at meaningful rates. The passing candidates treat the codebook as a tool they have already mapped, not a safety net they will figure out on test day.

Bottom line

Most journeyman electrician exams are open book with the NEC, Texas included, but a minority of states run closed-book or supplied-reference formats, so your state board's candidate bulletin is the only answer that counts. If your exam is open book, spend your prep hours on timed code navigation, tab the tables you use most, and confirm your book passes materials inspection before test day.
